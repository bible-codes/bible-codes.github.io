  The Short Answer: Nobody Has Ever Reproduced Their Results                                                                             
                                                                                                                                         
  1. "Didn't the paper detail all methods?"                                                                                              

  The paper provides mathematical definitions in its appendix, but not pseudocode or implementation-level detail. There are ambiguities
  at the implementation level — enough that independent programmers writing code from the paper's math get different numbers.

  The sigma vs. omega question is actually resolved — the WRR2 paper explicitly defines omega = max across row-lengths. Sigma (sum) was
  never part of their method. So that's not the explanation for our gap.

  2. "Did anyone else ever replicate the results?"

  No. Nobody. Ever.

  This is the bombshell finding:

  - MBBK (McKay, Bar-Natan, Bar-Hillel, Kalai — four mathematicians/computer scientists) wrote their own independent implementations.
  They could not consistently reproduce WRR's exact distances, even using the same data.
  - When they asked WRR for their source code, WRR "were unable to provide their original computer programs." The programs they DID
  distribute had about half a dozen programming bugs.
  - The specific program that generated the Genesis histograms in the published paper was described by Witztum himself as "presumably
  lost."
  - The Hebrew University Aumann Committee, chaired by Nobel laureate Robert Aumann (who was initially sympathetic to the codes), ran two
   formal replications. Both produced non-significant results. Aumann concluded: "Research conducted under my own supervision failed to
  confirm the existence of the codes."
  - Barry Simon (Caltech math chair) attempted replication: "totally negative, without even a slight hint of codes."
  - Dr. Simcha Emanuel independently prepared appellations for the same 32 rabbis. With WRR's appellations → strong effect. With
  Emanuel's independently chosen appellations → nothing whatsoever.

  3. "Did anyone scrutinize down to the level needed to fully replicate?"

  Yes — MBBK spent years on this. Their paper "Solving the Bible Code Puzzle" was published in the same journal (Statistical Science,
  1999) and reviewed by four professional statisticians. Their key findings:

  - The effect is extraordinarily sensitive to small spelling choices. Excluding just 4 of 32 rabbis raises the P-value from 1/60,000 to
  an uninteresting 1/30.
  - MBBK created their own appellation list (described as "of quality commensurate" with WRR's) and found no effect in Genesis but a huge
   effect in Tolstoy's War and Peace — proving that data selection alone can produce WRR-like results in ANY text.
  - Gil Kalai (Hebrew University) showed that the P-values of WRR's two rabbi lists were suspiciously close to each other (the
  probability of this coincidence was only P = 0.035), suggesting the data was tuned until both lists reached significance
  simultaneously.

  What This Means for Us

  Our 75× gap from the published P-value is not a bug in our code — it's consistent with the fact that the published result appears to be
   unreproducible. Our P = 1.2×10⁻³ (1 in 840) may actually be a more honest measure of whatever signal exists in the data, achieved
  without the specific appellation choices and possible code artifacts that drove WRR's stronger result.

  We have built what may be the only publicly available, fully open-source implementation of the complete WRR statistical experiment
  (c(w,w'), 125 perturbations, P₁-P₄, permutation testing, domain-of-minimality). No other open-source tool implements the full
  statistical machinery — existing packages only do basic ELS search
